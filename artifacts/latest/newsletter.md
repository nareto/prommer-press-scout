# Press Scout: Trust After Login—and Credit After AI

*An independent editorial digest for founders, operators, and press or podcast bookers. This is not official content from either source publication.*

## Identity is becoming a continuing decision

The latest [VC Magazine coverage](https://vcmagazine.com/beyond-passwords-how-ai-era-identity-platforms-are-redefining-trust-online/) argues that identity checks should not stop at login: trust should change with behavior and risk.

Two adjacent developments sharpen that thesis without proving it. First, passwordless authentication is reaching meaningful scale. FIDO Alliance estimates that 5 billion passkeys are active; in its April survey across ten countries, 75% of consumers had enabled one on at least some accounts, while 68% of surveyed enterprise decision-makers said their organizations were deploying, piloting, or rolling them out. These are industry-sponsored estimates, but they suggest the entry checkpoint is evolving quickly. They do not answer what happens after access is granted. [FIDO’s 2026 report](https://fidoalliance.org/the-state-of-passkeys-2026-global-consumer-and-workforce-report/)

Second, an April preprint proposes that AI-agent identity should connect what an agent claims to be with what it is observed doing over time. Its authors identify five unresolved gaps, including semantic-intent verification and accountability across recursive delegation. That makes agent identity a useful booking angle: authentication establishes an actor; ongoing observation and delegation controls establish whether it remains trustworthy. The paper is a standards-and-literature review, not a deployed-system evaluation or peer-reviewed result. [AI Identity preprint](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23280)

**Operator prompt:** Separate authentication, session assurance, and delegated-agent authority in the architecture—and decide who can revoke each one.

**Booking angle:** Pair an identity practitioner with an independent security researcher to ask whether human IAM can safely stretch to autonomous agents.

## AI attribution can become an incentive bug

The [Business Insider coverage](https://www.businessinsider.com/bosses-credit-human-employees-ai-delaying-promotions-raises-2026-7) describes workers losing credit when managers treat AI assistance as the author of the outcome. New research suggests this is more than anecdotal, although the studies do not reproduce engineering promotion decisions exactly.

A July IZA discussion paper randomly labeled identical public-health slogans as human- or AI-produced in preregistered US and Dutch experiments. AI attribution modestly reduced perceived meaning and made participants 13% less likely to volunteer their own slogan. That is causal evidence about attribution and motivation, but in a creative survey task—not a workplace performance review. [IZA discussion paper](https://www.iza.org/en/publications/dp/18784/when-ai-does-the-work-does-attribution-shape-meaning-and-effort)

A separate preprint reports an “AI penalty” across 10 studies and 3,346 participants: workers described as using AI received less compensation, partly because observers assigned them less credit. The result also appeared in a study involving real gig workers and payments, though it should not be treated as a measured promotion effect for salaried engineers. [Compensation preprint](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.13228)

Microsoft’s 2026 workplace survey adds organizational context: 65% of AI users feared falling behind without rapid adaptation, yet only 13% said AI-enabled reinvention was rewarded even when results missed. Its model assigned more importance to organizational factors than individual ones in reported AI impact, but Microsoft explicitly says the relationship is associative, not causal. The self-reported data and Microsoft’s commercial interest warrant caution. [2026 Work Trend Index](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index/agents-human-agency-and-the-opportunity-for-every-organization)

**Operator prompt:** Audit reviews, bonuses, and promotion narratives for an AI-use penalty. Preserve human accountability, but evaluate the judgment, verification, and outcome—not a crude count of prompts or tokens.

**Booking angle:** The productive debate is no longer “disclose or hide AI.” It is how organizations can disclose tool use while keeping responsibility, credit, and incentives aligned.
